# FOIA / Public Records Toolkit – How to Use These Templates

This folder contains practical tools for investigating how agencies use **neurotechnology, behavioral programs, and psychological practices**, especially on vulnerable populations (people experiencing homelessness, people with substance use disorders, and people in carceral or mandated treatment systems).

It is written with **Arizona** in mind, but the structure works in any U.S. jurisdiction with freedom of information or public‑records laws.

## 1. Know your legal hook

### Arizona (state & local agencies)

Arizona's Public Records Law is primarily found in **A.R.S. § 39‑121 et seq.**

- A.R.S. § 39‑121: “Public records and other matters in the custody of any officer shall be open to inspection by any person at all times during office hours.”  
- Title 39 includes additional sections on access, exemptions, and procedures.

Key points:

- You do **not** have to be a lawyer, journalist, or Arizona resident to request records.
- Requests must **reasonably describe** the records sought; they do not need special legal language.
- Agencies have a duty to respond within a **reasonable time**, though there is no fixed number of days in the statute.

For federal agencies (CIA, DOJ, etc.), you use the **federal FOIA** instead:

- FOIA requires a written request that reasonably describes the records and is sent to the correct agency’s FOIA office.
- Most federal agencies accept requests by web form or email.

Always check current guides for your jurisdiction:

- Arizona: public records law booklets, state agency public‑info pages, or press freedom orgs.
- Federal: FOIA.gov “How to make a FOIA request.”

## 2. Choose the right target agencies

Before sending a request, decide **who** is likely to have the records you want.

For neurotech + psychological practices involving vulnerable populations, high‑value targets include:

- **City police departments** (e.g., Phoenix Police Department)
- **County sheriff’s offices** (jails, transport, patrol)
- **Departments of corrections** (state prisons, community supervision)
- **Health departments** (mental health and substance‑use services)
- **Courts and specialty courts** (drug courts, mental‑health courts)
- **Homeless services agencies** (city/county homelessness offices, shelter operators)
- **State technology / innovation offices** (if they fund “smart” systems, predictive analytics, or digital therapeutics)

In many cases, you will want to send **similar requests** to several agencies so that responses can be compared.

## 3. Overview of the templates in this folder

- `foia-cover-letter-template.md`  
  General cover letter that frames your request around neurotechnology, psychological practices, and vulnerable populations, with placeholders for your name, agency, and date.

- `foia-request-neurotechnology.md`  
  Focused on **EEG/BCI devices, neuromodulation, VR/AR systems, and digital therapeutics**, plus policies and projects that use them.

- `foia-request-psychological-practices.md`  
  Focused on **sleep‑deprivation, environmental/sensory manipulation, and behavior‑modification programs**, including related legal opinions and complaints.

- `foia-request-vulnerable-populations.md`  
  Focused on programs and technologies targeting **people experiencing homelessness, substance‑use disorders, and mandated treatment**, and how these groups are policed and managed.

You can mix and match: for a first pass, consider sending **all three** request templates with the cover letter to one agency, then adjust based on what you learn.

## 4. How to customize a request

1. **Make a copy** of each template you plan to use.  
   - Example: copy `foia-cover-letter-template.md` to `my-foia-phoenix-pd-2026-01.md`.

2. Fill in all `[bracketed placeholders]`.  
   - `[Requestor Name]`, `[Agency Name]`, `[Start Date]`, `[End Date]`, `[Dollar Amount You Are Willing to Pay]`, etc.

3. Narrow or broaden the date range.  
   - For “new tech,” a range like `2018‑01‑01` to `present` can be effective.
   - For behavior policies, consider going back further if you suspect long‑standing practices.

4. Tune for your jurisdiction.  
   - In Arizona, keep the A.R.S. citations.
   - In other states, swap the cited statutes for your state’s public records law references.

5. Decide on **scope level**:
   - If you want a **broad map**, leave the language as written and be prepared for a large volume of records and/or a request to narrow.
   - If you want **something specific**, add keywords (e.g., a known vendor name, project codename, or facility).

## 5. Sending your request

### Identify the correct contact

- Many agencies list a **Public Records Officer** or **FOIA Officer** on their website.
- For Arizona state agencies, there may be an online form or a dedicated email.

If you cannot find a specific contact, address it to “Public Records Officer” at the agency’s main mailing address and email.

### Delivery methods

- **Email**: preferred, as it’s easy to track and reply to.
- **Web form**: some agencies require this; copy‑paste your letter into the form.
- **Mail**: send via certified mail if you need proof of delivery.

Always **save a copy** of what you sent and note the date.

## 6. Tracking responses

Create a simple tracking file (spreadsheet or markdown) with:

- Date sent
- Agency
- Requests included (e.g., “#1 Neurotech, #2 Psychological Practices”)
- Contact email or portal
- Status: “Sent”, “Acknowledged”, “Partial response”, “Completed”, “Appeal”, etc.
- Notes: key points, deadlines the agency provided, any fees quoted.

When an agency responds:

- Save **all correspondence**.
- If they ask you to narrow the request, try to **negotiate clarity, not surrender**:
  - Offer to prioritize certain items (e.g., policies first, then contracts).
  - Ask them to suggest search terms or date ranges that are easier to process while still meaningful.

## 7. Handling denials, delays, and fees

### Denials / Exemptions

If the agency denies records or redacts heavily:

- Ask for:
  - The specific statute cited for each redaction or withholding.
  - A description of the withheld material (e.g., “policy memo, 5 pages”).

- Consider:
  - Filing an **administrative appeal** if your jurisdiction allows it.
  - Contacting press‑freedom or transparency organizations for help.
  - In Arizona, resources exist for drafting complaints or seeking judicial review if the denial appears unlawful.

### Delays

- Politely remind the agency of the request and ask for an estimated completion date.
- In Arizona, the law requires a **prompt** or **reasonable** response; lengthy silence can sometimes be challenged.

### Fees

- The templates ask for a **fee waiver or reduction**, emphasizing public interest.
- If fees are high:
  - Ask for an itemized estimate.
  - Narrow the request (e.g., shorter date range, fewer facilities).
  - Consider crowdsourcing or support from a civil‑society group.

## 8. Using the results

When records arrive:

- Organize them by type: policies, contracts, emails, complaints, etc.
- Look for:
  - References to neurotechnology vendors, VR/AR projects, or “digital therapeutics”.
  - Explicit policies on sleep‑deprivation, lighting, noise, or behavioral programs.
  - Mentions of homeless encampments, addiction programs, or experimental initiatives.
- Cross‑reference:
  - Records from multiple agencies to spot inconsistencies or missing pieces.
  - Public reports (e.g., DOJ findings, neurorights analyses) to see whether local practice aligns with national concerns.

If you find something significant:

- Consider sharing it with:
  - Local journalists
  - Civil‑liberties / neurorights organizations
  - Lawmakers and oversight bodies

## 9. Safety and ethics

- Do **not** include confidential personal health or legal information in your public records requests.
- If you are a current or former employee, consider your own legal obligations and whistleblower protections before sharing internal documents.
- If someone’s experience is part of your motivation (including your own), focus your requests on **programs and policies**, not on specific individuals.

## 10. Adapting this toolkit

This toolkit is meant to be:

- **Forkable**: you can adapt it for other states or countries by changing legal references and agency names.
- **Extendable**: add new request templates (e.g., for federal FOIA, vendor‑specific inquiries, or particular facilities).
- **Collaborative**: pool responses from multiple users to build a bigger picture of how neurotechnology and psychological practices are being used across jurisdictions.

If you improve or localize these templates, consider contributing back so others can benefit.
