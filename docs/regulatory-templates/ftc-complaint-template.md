## I. Introduction and Summary of Allegations

This complaint alleges that [Respondent Name], a [business type] operating [product/platform name], has engaged in unfair and/or deceptive acts or practices in violation of Section 5 of the Federal Trade Commission Act, 15 U.S.C. § 45, through its collection, use, and sharing of neural data.

**Core Allegations**:
1. [Respondent] collects neural data from consumers without providing clear, conspicuous, and specific notice of the scope and purpose of such collection.
2. [Respondent] obtains consent through dark patterns or pre-checked boxes that do not constitute affirmative, granular, and revocable permission as required by emerging state neural-data laws and human-rights norms.
3. [Respondent] repurposes neural data for unrelated commercial uses (e.g., advertising, AI training) without obtaining separate, informed consent, violating the principle of purpose limitation.
4. [Respondent] employs neural data to infer emotional or cognitive states for subliminal manipulation of user behavior in virtual environments, creating significant risk of mental integrity harm.
5. [Respondent] fails to implement reasonable security measures to protect highly sensitive neural data from unauthorized access, use, or disclosure.

These practices cause substantial injury to consumers that is not reasonably avoidable by consumers themselves and not outweighed by countervailing benefits, meeting the FTC's definition of "unfair" practices. The misrepresentations regarding data collection and use constitute "deceptive" practices under FTC precedent.

## III. Legal Analysis

### A. Violation of Section 5 of the FTC Act: Unfair Practices
Under 15 U.S.C. § 45(n), a practice is unfair if it:
1. Causes or is likely to cause substantial injury to consumers;
2. The injury is not reasonably avoidable by consumers themselves; and
3. The injury is not outweighed by countervailing benefits to consumers or competition.

[Respondent]'s practices meet all three prongs:

1. **Substantial Injury**: Neural data reveals intimate aspects of mental life. Unauthorized collection, use, or disclosure can cause dignitary harm, emotional distress, discrimination, and loss of cognitive autonomy. The sensitivity of this data amplifies the injury compared to conventional personal information.

2. **Not Reasonably Avoidable**: Consumers cannot reasonably avoid these practices because:
   - Notice is buried in lengthy terms of service or presented via dark patterns
   - Consent mechanisms do not allow granular control over data uses
   - Technical complexity prevents meaningful understanding of neural-data risks
   - Network effects and lack of alternatives limit meaningful choice

3. **No Outweighing Benefits**: Any efficiency gains from neural-data exploitation do not outweigh the fundamental harms to mental privacy and cognitive liberty, especially when less intrusive alternatives exist (e.g., on-device processing, anonymization, strict purpose limitation).

### B. Violation of Section 5 of the FTC Act: Deceptive Practices
A representation, omission, or practice is deceptive if it is material and likely to mislead a reasonable consumer acting reasonably under the circumstances.

[Respondent] has engaged in deception through:
- **Misleading Privacy Representations**: Stating that data is "anonymized" or "aggregated" while retaining re-identification capabilities or using inferred traits for individual profiling.
- **Omission of Material Facts**: Failing to disclose that neural data is used for AI training, sold to third parties, or retained indefinitely.
- **Dark Pattern Consent Flows**: Designing interfaces that nudge users toward over-sharing through pre-checked boxes, confusing language, or penalty defaults.

These misrepresentations are material because they concern the core privacy expectations of consumers using neurotechnology.

### C. Emerging Legal Standards as Evidence of Unfairness
While not binding on the FTC, emerging legal standards provide strong evidence of societal consensus regarding unfair practices:

1. **State Neural-Data Laws**: Colorado and California classify neural data as "sensitive personal information," requiring explicit consent and purpose limitation. [Respondent]'s practices violate these standards in jurisdictions where they apply, indicating a broader pattern of unfairness.

2. **EU AI Act**: Prohibits AI systems using "significantly harmful subliminal manipulation" and bans emotion recognition in workplaces/schools. [Respondent]'s use of neural data for behavioral nudging in virtual environments aligns with prohibited practices under this gold-standard framework.

3. **Human-Rights Doctrine**: International instruments recognize mental privacy and freedom of thought as fundamental rights. Practices that coerce, manipulate, or exploit neural data contravene these norms, reinforcing their characterization as unfair.

## IV. Requested Relief

The Federal Trade Commission is respectfully requested to:

1. **Investigate** [Respondent]'s neural data practices pursuant to Section 6(b) of the FTC Act.
2. **Issue a Complaint** alleging violations of Section 5 of the FTC Act.
3. **Seek Preliminary and Permanent Injunctions** requiring [Respondent] to:
   - Cease collecting neural data without clear, conspicuous, and specific notice
   - Implement granular, affirmative, and revocable consent mechanisms
   - Prohibit repurposing of neural data without separate consent
   - Halt use of neural data for subliminal manipulation or high-stakes inference
   - Delete previously collected neural data obtained through unfair/deceptive practices
4. **Impose Civil Penalties** for knowing violations of final Commission orders.
5. **Require Regular Compliance Reporting** and independent audits of neural-data practices.
6. **Publish Guidance** on unfair/deceptive practices in neurotechnology to provide industry clarity.
